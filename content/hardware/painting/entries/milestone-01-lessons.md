# Lessons Learned

Take these with a grain of salt, given that Milestone 1 represents approximately 3 painting projects under my belt.

## Paints

Hobby paint is probably nice, but [99 bottles of paint on the wall](https://a.co/d/6mutCAi) for $50 is a great starting point. Additionally, you don't NEED to buy special [wash paint, highlight paint, airbrush paint], but having some will help you make your own.

For example, I bought a [3-pack](https://a.co/d/0NcqqXE) of primer without realizing it was Ready-To-Spray, but I'm glad I did because it showed me the consistency I needed for the airbrush. That HissiCo paint is THICC, which is great because a little lasts a long time, but if I hadn't had the primer I'd have probably never gotten it to the right flow rate (it ended up being nearly 1:1 ratio).

That said, if you DON'T get the [wash, highlight, airbrush] paint, expect to need to get some ingredients to make your own.

### Washes

"Wash" paint is for making shadows/dark areas, so you'll need to mix your colors with a TOUCH of black or whatever you want to use to darken, THEN you'll need to thin it out so that you apply even coats over large areas to allow the pigment to settle into grooves and features.

To thin it, you're going to need something like [Pouring Medium](https://a.co/d/afn6ukz) or "Flow Medium". According to the internet, these are two different things, but that Amazon link doesn't care about the internet's opinion and mixes the terms freely. I can attest that it did thin my paint and allow it to act like a wash, though.

I actually ended up over-thinning and under-darkening, which, now that I'm done, I recommend as a starting point. Adding a little extra pigment with a paint brush instead of trying to pull paint OFF the figure was easy and let me dial in the color/temperature and thickness without worrying too much.

### Highlights

I just mixed a little white into my main color on my wet pallet and applied it where I wanted to bring out a highlight... I think is was probably the weakest aspect of Milestone 1, if I'm being honest. I need to do a little practice/research on dry brushing and highlight techniques.

## Airbrushing

### Tighten. All. The. Things.

You don't have to break the parts, but EVERYTHING should be tightened down. Your needle should be flush against the nozzle, your paint cup should be tight against your brush, your air hose... You get it. Every gap that air or paint can escape through, it will, and cleaning up a leak is 1) No fun 2) Time you're not painting.

### Wear a Glove

You don't really need _both_ hands gloved, but the one holding whatever you're painting should have a glove on. One reason is so that you don't have to worry about over-spray, but the other reason is cleaning.

### Cleaning

All the ads and videos and stuff make cleaning the airbrush seem easy, and I think the reality is slightly more complex.

Think about painting with a brush - every minute or so, you're rinsing your brush, drying, dipping, and resuming. With an airbrush, though, you'd be dumping your paint cup every time, so instead you paint for longer.

But the paint still dries out - not in the cup, but at the tip of the brush. The higher your air pressure, the more likely the tip is to stay clean, but that's where your problems will crop up first.

I've developed a bit of a rhythm where I fill the cup ~3/4 of the way (I mix the paint in a separate cup, see [The Painter's Portal](https://youtu.be/T-oQ1uMZYZA?si=uII7n2khZhzR566H) for a quick run down), spray until it's empty, then do a "Quick rinse."

#### Quick Rinse Clean

I bought a box of little [glass bottles](https://a.co/d/aPJ8j0t) that I can keep in a [Gridfinity](#hardware/3d/ocd) box, but you can use whatever you want to have:

- A clean bottle of water
- A bottle of [Acetone](https://a.co/d/ceSufc7) (I like this bottle 'cause the top is the same size as my paint bottles, so I can swap on an old one to make pouring easier... I don't know exactly what type of plastic they're made of, though, so be careful if you try it)
- A little [Pipette/Dropper](https://a.co/d/gJzfJiA) for each one
- An [Airbrush Cleaning Kit](https://a.co/d/8b3fkKY)

1. Using the water dropper, add water to the cup and spray it into the cleaning pot until there's no more liquid paint
2. Take the top off of the cleaning pot
3. Add a little water to the cup
4. Maybe put the lid on? That's probably the right way to do this, but just in case:
5. **POINT THE CUP AWAY FROM YOU OR ANYTHING YOU CARE ABOUT**
6. Cover the nozzle with your gloved finger and spray
    - The air should be blocked and be pushed back into the cup, causing bubbles and a mess
7. Swear a little at the mess, then dump the contents of the cup into the cleaning pot
8. Repeat Steps 3-7 until the cup is clean after you make your mess
9. Put the top back on the cleaning pot

Check the tip of the airbrush - if it's got paint on it, clean it with a little pick in the kit BUT DON'T HURT THE NEEDLE. It's a delicate balance.

Now you've gotta make a call - is there enough dry paint in the cup that it's going to mess up your next color or spray? Are you actually done for the day? If so, it's time for...

#### Deep Clean

Assuming you've done your Quick Rinse, now it's time for chemicals. Most of the paint should be out of the cup and nozzle, but to get the last bits, you're going to need to use the Acetone dropper, a paper towel, and the cleaning pot.

```flare
**YOU'RE GOING TO BE AEROSOLIZING A TOXIC CHEMICAL**

Use a respirator, ventilation, the filter of your cleaning pot, etc.
```

Use the dropper to add acetone to your paint cup and, using the gloved hand, wipe it down with the paper towel.

Add more acetone, and spray it into the cleaning pot. You shouldn't need to do the whole "cover the nozzle and spray acetone everywhere," but if you do, use the cover and whatever safety precautions you can.

Once the cup is clean and you've run some acetone through the sprayer, add some acetone to a shallow cup.

Then, **`very`** **carefully** (seriously, all the stuff that can go terribly wrong is in the following steps, _NOW_ is the time to be careful):

1. Remove the nozzle, **don't bend the needle**
2. Remove any o-ring on the end of the nozzle and **set it somewhere safe**
3. Place the nozzle into the acetone and swish it around **GENTLY**
4. **After the next step, your trigger will be loose**
5. While the nozzle soaks, loosen the rear needle retainer and slide the needle `FORWARD`, through the front of the brush
6. Wipe down the needle with a little acetone and **set it somewhere safe**
7. Use the brush in the cleaning kit to scrub off any persistent paint from the front of the brush **WHILE NOT LOSING YOUR TRIGGER**
    - Be careful - the brush is gonna make 💩 fly everywhere
8. Use the brush to scrub off any persistent paint from the nozzle
9. **Put the O-ring back on the nozzle back on**
10. Re-attach the nozzle, **tightly**
11. Insert the needle through the back of the brush, ensuring you **don't bend it** and you **go through the gap in the trigger**, and seat it tightly against the nozzle
12. Tighten the rear needle retainer

Breathe. Well, not the acetone, breath something else.
