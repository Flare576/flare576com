# Airbrush Saga Pt. 2

# The Hunt

As I explained in [Part 1](#hardware/painting/first-airbrush), I needed a quiet air compressor, and that really means one of two things:

1. I build a sound-dampening box that _also_ lets enough air in to cool the compressor **AND** provide air to actually compress
2. I buy a tiny little electric air compressor

I found a video by [Saida Props](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=MDIuEDJAWZI) where he talks about several air compressors, including one of the USB-powered units available on Amazon. He does a fantastic job breaking down the effectiveness and utility of each one, and his thoughts on the "NoName Cordless 2024" were:

```Saida
It has some big flaws, like significantly reduced airflow when the battery is at 50%.

But It actually works exceptionally well for certain air brushes...
```

So, it's viable, which was what I was hoping to hear, because the flip-side of the equation is that there are 20 different products on Amazon alone that follow this form factor, or one very similar to it.

And none of them have 100% satisfied customers.

# The Gamble

Anytime you're ordering something like this from a online retailer, you're taking a chance.

By "like this," I mean:

1. A consumer electronic that is _also_ a consumer mechanical device
2. Something that has been commodified to the point where there are several knock-offs
3. There's no single "Reliable" brand from which everyone is copying.

So, what I did was find the product with the most reviews and estimated my chances of getting a bad one in the mail.

I found the [Gocheer Airbrush Kit with Air Compressor...](https://a.co/d/5eIf53F), which had over 4,00 reviews with a 4.2 ⭐ rating (~82% 4+ stars).

To me, that means I've got about an 18% chance of getting a broken piece of garbage in the mail.

I knew how the thing worked (Saida actually took his apart to show its insides) and this one was NOT battery powered (one less potential point of failure), so I figured at worst I could fiddle with it if it was dead on arrival, and then send it back for another shot (thanks to Amazon's return policy).

What I got in the mail was actually top-friggen-notch.

The compressor works a treat, and with a [little](https://a.co/d/8wew3Q3) [tweaking](https://a.co/d/hljwzG6) it even works with my Dad's old airbrush! _And_ with a little _additional_ [tweaking](https://a.co/d/5bvJ3T3), the new airbrush ALSO works with my old air compressor.

**ANNNNNNDD** with a little _extra additional_ [tweaking](https://a.co/d/7GRHdSR), the new compressor works via a [battery pack](https://a.co/d/9pNn5k9)!

The new airbrush's trigger is a little stiff for my weak-ass pointer finger, but essentially every component of the kit is exactly as advertised.

## Additional Purchases

To make painting inside a little easier during the winter, I splurged and got a [paint station](https://a.co/d/8pQAiLC) with an exhaust. This was definitely the least-necessary thing I bought - all the paint I ended up getting are Non-Toxic and I don't plan to use any rattle cans for a while - but it makes me feel better about spraying in my little office with the door closed when I'm using the normal compressor.

> Note: I also looked into making one of these myself, but the TL;DR version is that any fans that can move enough air to make a DIY version "safe" are going to probably set you back at least half of what the Vevor costs, and that's not including the duct work and printing time/materials.

I also needed [some paint](https://a.co/d/5LpfIJj), and while I could have scrounged some from my artist-wife, the little part of my brain that demands compartmentalized kits would have screamed at me every time I had to step out of my office for a new color. Plus, I got to make this:
