# Goal

Sometimes, hand-sanding is necessary - when finishing a piece, or working with small spaces, for example.

However, it's a slow, painful process that I like to avoid if I can. There are a LOT of sanding tools ([belt sanders](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Belt_sander) and [orbital sanders](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Sander) and [spindle sanders](https://www.harborfreight.com/14-in-oscillating-spindle-sander-58546.html) and...), but I wanted something smaller for 3D prints.

If only there was a small, hand-held tool that spins stuff...

# College Flare

One of the first tools I bought myself was a [Dremel](https://a.co/d/9mJ7nbH) rotary tool.

Obviously not that specific one, since mine was probably purchased at a defunct brick-and-mortar store before Amazon existed.

I still have it, and it still works well, but it's lowest speed is 5,000 RPM, so words like _gently_ and _carefully_ don't really enter into its lexicon. It's better at cutting plastic than polishing it.

## Go Bigger To Go Slower

I ended up picking up a [rotary tool](https://a.co/d/5E9qbL2) on Amazon. The motor hangs from a [rail hook](https://a.co/d/94gEemR) so the position of the flex shaft is super... flexible.

But the main reason I got this specific unit is that it has a foot-pedal speed control. Push on the pedal hard, and you can get it up to 23,000 RPM. But, if you barely push on it, you can get it as low as 500 RPM (or even slower if you feather<sup>1</sup> it).

But, the tool by itself is useless - it needs attachments, and it didn't come with a sweet box like my Dremel did...

> <sup>1</sup>: I just realized a term I grew up with probably isn't common knowledge. When I was young, my dad took my brother and I to a local roller skating rink that the owner converted to do remote control car racing every Sunday night. One of the things he taught us about cornering was to "feather" the throttle.
>
> Essentially, the problem with cornering is two-fold:
>
> 1. It's really hard to know the EXACT speed that you need to go to make it around a corner without flipping _and_ to still do it quickly
> 2. That speed changes as you round the corner
>
> So, you don't stay at one position on the throttle. You alternate between "too fast" (pull the trigger hard) and "too slow" (release it slightly) rapidly.
>
> That's "feathering" the throttle.
